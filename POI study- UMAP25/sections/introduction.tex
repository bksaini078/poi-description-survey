\section{Introduction}
Travel planning increasingly happens through digital platforms that help users evaluate accommodations, attractions, and other Points of Interest (POIs). Recommender systems have therefore become central in tourism applications, where personalization is used to cope with information overload and heterogeneous user needs \cite{adomavicius_toward_2005,renjith_extensive_2020}. In the POI domain specifically, prior work has largely emphasized predicting which places users may prefer (e.g., next-POI or ranked POI recommendation) and how to incorporate richer signals such as context and heterogeneous data \cite{wang_survey_2025,Li2024SIGIR_LLMNextPOI}. Recent surveys further argue that large language models (LLMs) are becoming an enabling component across the recommendation pipeline, including user interaction and explanation generation \cite{Zhao2024TKDE_LLMRecSysEra,wang_embracing_2024,wang_point--interest_2025}.

However, while ranking and recommendation determine \emph{which} POIs are shown, users still rely on POI \emph{descriptions} to decide \emph{why} a place is relevant and whether it is credible and worth visiting. In many systems, these descriptions remain static and generic, presenting the same framing to all readers despite differences in interests, travel styles, and constraints. This “one-size-fits-all” presentation can under-emphasize what matters most to a given user (e.g., historical context versus family suitability), potentially limiting perceived clarity and usefulness. Generative AI offers a way to adapt the \emph{emphasis} of descriptions at scale, complementing POI recommendation and supporting more user-centered travel decision-making \cite{li_generative_2025,zhang_co-creating_2024}.

At the same time, deploying LLM-generated travel content raises practical concerns around information quality and trust, especially in domains where credibility is critical. Tourism-focused GenAI research has begun to examine opportunities and risks (e.g., reliability, domain adaptation, and user acceptance), and approaches such as retrieval augmentation have been proposed to mitigate factual errors \cite{hsu_fine-tuned_2024,qi_rag-optimized_2025,li_generative_2025}. Motivated by this gap, this study examines whether LLM-based personalization of POI descriptions can improve perceived information quality without harming trust or engagement. A prototype was developed that presents users with original (manual) descriptions and LLM-adapted variants conditioned on stated preferences, and both versions were evaluated in a within-subjects study across multiple POIs. The contributions of this study are (1) an empirical comparison of manual versus LLM-adapted POI descriptions using significance, trustworthiness, and clarity as evaluation dimensions, and (2) evidence about user acceptance of adaptive description personalization in a tourism context.
