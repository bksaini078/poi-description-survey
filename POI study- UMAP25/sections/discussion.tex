\section{Discussion}
This study examined whether LLM-based personalization of POI descriptions changes how people judge the quality of travel information. Aggregated results show improved clarity ratings for AI-adapted descriptions, with borderline evidence of improved trustworthiness ($p = 0.050$). Across measures, AI adaptation primarily reduced neutral responses and increased positive categories. This pattern is consistent with increased perceived relevance: when descriptions foreground details aligned with a reader's interests, the same factual content can appear easier to process and more useful.

Effect sizes were small (Cramer's $V$ in the 0.07 to 0.11 range), suggesting modest practical gains. In addition, statistical tests were computed on pooled ratings, while the study design includes repeated judgments by the same participant across POIs. Outcomes should therefore be interpreted as descriptive evidence of distributional shifts rather than definitive estimates. When considering multiple outcomes (significance, trustworthiness, clarity), uncorrected $p$-values should be treated cautiously.

From a practical perspective, these findings support the use of LLMs to adjust emphasis and framing in POI micro-content using lightweight preference signals. This approach can scale personalization without extensive manual editing. Platforms should treat transparency and disclosure as design choices, setting clear expectations without unnecessarily signaling low quality.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}
Several limitations should be acknowledged. Participants evaluated descriptions in a controlled setting rather than during real-world trip planning, limiting ecological validity. The study measured perceived outcomes but not downstream behavior such as booking rates or actual visits. The within-subjects design may introduce demand characteristics, and the sample composition limits generalizability to other demographics and cultural contexts. Future work should expand participant diversity, conduct longitudinal field studies tracking actual behavior, and test finer personalization factors (trip purpose, companions, budget). Investigating how transparency about AI involvement affects trust, and balancing personalized relevance with serendipitous discovery, remain open questions.
