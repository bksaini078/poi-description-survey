\section{Discussion}
This study examined whether LLM-based personalization of POI descriptions changes how people judge the quality of travel information. Overall, participants rated the AI-adapted descriptions as clearer than the manual baseline descriptions. Ratings of trustworthiness were largely similar across conditions, with a small tendency for AI-adapted text to be evaluated more positively. Participants' choices of which description they would prefer to read were also broadly stable. Taken together, these results suggest that personalization can increase perceived clarity without reducing willingness to engage with the content.

Across measures, AI-adapted descriptions more often received positive ratings rather than neutral ratings. A likely reason is increased \emph{perceived relevance}. When a description highlights details that match a reader's interests, the same facts may feel more useful and easier to understand. This interpretation fits prior work on tailored communication, where framing and emphasis influence how information is processed even when the underlying content is unchanged. At the same time, the mostly stable trust ratings suggest that rewriting alone may not strongly change credibility judgments in a low-stakes reading task.

From a practical perspective, these findings support the use of LLMs to adjust emphasis and framing in POI micro-content using lightweight preference signals (e.g., stated interests). This approach can help scale personalization without requiring extensive manual editing. However, trust is sensitive to context and presentation. Platforms should therefore treat transparency and disclosure as design choices and aim to set clear expectations without unnecessarily signaling low quality.

\subsection{Limitations}
Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, participants evaluated descriptions in a controlled study setting rather than during real-world trip planning, which may limit ecological validity. Second, the study focused on perceived outcomes (e.g., clarity and trustworthiness) and did not measure downstream behavior such as click-through, saving, itinerary inclusion, or actual visits. Third, the within-subjects design supports direct comparison but may increase attention to differences between versions and introduce demand characteristics. Fourth, the study did not test potential downsides of personalization, such as reduced serendipity or over-fitting to stated interests. Finally, the sample composition may limit generalizability to other age groups, levels of technical familiarity, and cultural contexts.
