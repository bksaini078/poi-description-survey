\section{Discussion}
This study examined whether LLM-based personalization of POI descriptions changes how people judge the quality of travel information. Paired statistical analyses revealed that AI-adapted descriptions received significantly higher ratings for both clarity and trustworthiness, while perceived significance remained comparable between conditions. One possible explanation is increased perceived relevance: when descriptions foreground details aligned with a reader's interests, the same factual content may appear easier to process and more credible. However, other mechanisms such as writing style differences or content organization may also contribute to these effects.

Although the differences in mean ratings were modest in absolute terms, the consistency of improvement across both clarity and trustworthiness, combined with comparable engagement ratings, suggests that personalization may provide benefits without introducing obvious drawbacks.

From a practical perspective, these preliminary findings suggest potential for using LLMs to adjust emphasis and framing in POI micro-content using lightweight preference signals. This approach may help scale personalization without extensive manual editing. The improvement in trustworthiness is particularly noteworthy, as it addresses a common concern about AI-generated content~\cite{li_generative_2025}. Platforms implementing such systems may consider transparency and disclosure as design choices. While this study did not manipulate transparency (participants were unaware which descriptions were AI-generated), future work should investigate how disclosure affects perceived quality and trust.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}
Several limitations should be acknowledged. The controlled study setting may not fully reflect real-world travel planning, and we measured participants' perceptions rather than actual decisions (e.g., whether they would book a place). The sample composition (67\% male, two nationalities, convenience sampling) limits generalizability, and results are based on a single LLM with default settings. Effect sizes, though significant, were small. Future work should expand participant diversity, conduct field studies tracking actual behavior, and test finer personalization factors (trip purpose, companions, budget). Investigating transparency about AI involvement and balancing personalized relevance with serendipitous discovery remain open questions.
